Here's the full mathematical treatment of the SISP physical layer — signal model, channel impairments, link budget, coding, and PER analysis — written as a formal simulation reference document, followed by an interactive BER/PER curve tool.

## SISP Physical Layer — Full Signal & Noise Model

---

### 1. The Transmitted Signal Model

SISP uses **BPSK** (Binary Phase Shift Keying). The continuous-time passband signal from satellite A is:

$$s(t) = \sqrt{2P_{tx}} \cdot b(t) \cdot \cos(2\pi f_c t + \varphi_0)$$

where $b(t) \in \{-1, +1\}$ is the NRZ baseband bit stream (each bit has duration $T_b = 1/R_b$, with $R_b$ the bit rate), $f_c = 26 \text{ GHz}$ (Ka-band carrier), and $\varphi_0$ is the initial phase offset.

The **complex baseband equivalent** (used in simulation) is:

$$\tilde{s}(t) = \sqrt{P_{tx}} \cdot b(t) \cdot e^{j\varphi_0}$$

The **energy per bit** is:

$$E_b = P_{tx} \cdot T_b = \frac{P_{tx}}{R_b}$$

For $P_{tx} = 1\text{ W}$, $R_b = 100\text{ kbps}$: $E_b = 10^{-5}$ J = $-50$ dBJ.

---

### 2. The Channel — Three Independent Impairments

#### 2.1 Free-Space Path Loss (Friis equation)

The received signal power at distance $d$ is given by the **Friis transmission equation**:

$$P_r = P_{tx} \cdot G_{tx} \cdot G_{rx} \cdot \left(\frac{\lambda}{4\pi d}\right)^2 = P_{tx} \cdot G_{tx} \cdot G_{rx} \cdot \frac{c^2}{(4\pi d f_c)^2}$$

In decibels, the **free-space path loss** $L_{fs}$:

$$L_{fs}(\text{dB}) = 20\log_{10}(d) + 20\log_{10}(f_c) + 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{4\pi}{c}\right)$$

Computing term by term at $d = 1000\text{ km}$, $f_c = 26\text{ GHz}$:

$$L_{fs} = \underbrace{20\log_{10}(10^6)}_{=120\text{ dB}} + \underbrace{20\log_{10}(2.6\times10^{10})}_{=194.3\text{ dB}} + \underbrace{20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{4\pi}{3\times10^8}\right)}_{=-169.5\text{ dB}} = 144.8\text{ dB}$$

The **distance law**: every doubling of $d$ adds $+6$ dB of loss. From $d = 500\text{ km}$ to $d = 2000\text{ km}$, loss increases by $+12$ dB.

#### 2.2 Doppler Shift

Two LEO satellites at $v_1 \approx v_2 \approx 7.8\text{ km/s}$ in non-coplanar orbits have a relative radial velocity $v_r$. The received frequency:

$$f_r = f_c \cdot \frac{c + v_{r,rx}}{c - v_{r,tx}} \approx f_c\left(1 + \frac{v_r}{c}\right)$$

The **Doppler shift** $\Delta f$:

$$\Delta f = f_c \cdot \frac{v_r}{c}$$

At Ka-band ($f_c = 26\text{ GHz}$), for $v_r = 1\text{ km/s}$ (typical non-coplanar LEO pair):

$$\Delta f = 26\times10^9 \times \frac{1000}{3\times10^8} = 86.7\text{ kHz}$$

This is significant — it is nearly the full signal bandwidth. The received signal after Doppler:

$$r_{\text{Doppler}}(t) = \sqrt{P_r}\cdot b\!\left(t - \tau\right)\cdot\cos\!\Big(2\pi(f_c + \Delta f)t + \varphi\Big)$$

For simulation, you model the **time-varying** Doppler because $v_r(t)$ changes as the satellites orbit:

$$\Delta f(t) = \frac{f_c}{c}\cdot\dot{d}(t)$$

where $\dot{d}(t) = \frac{d}{dt}|\mathbf{r}_A(t) - \mathbf{r}_B(t)|$ is the range rate from your orbit propagator (SGP4/Orekit, Module M1).

**Doppler pre-compensation**: Since both satellites know each other's approximate ephemeris, A can pre-shift its transmitted frequency by $-\Delta f_{\text{predicted}}$ before transmission. Residual Doppler after pre-compensation is then only the ephemeris prediction error — typically $< 1\text{ kHz}$ — well within the receiver's tracking loop bandwidth.

#### 2.3 Thermal Noise

The received noise is modeled as **AWGN** (Additive White Gaussian Noise). Noise power in bandwidth $B$:

$$N = k_B \cdot T_{sys} \cdot B$$

where $k_B = 1.38\times10^{-23}\text{ J/K}$ (Boltzmann), $T_{sys}$ is the **system noise temperature**:

$$T_{sys} = T_{ant} + T_{LNA} + T_{cable}$$

For a Ka-band CubeSat receiver: $T_{ant} \approx 30\text{ K}$ (cold sky), $T_{LNA} \approx 200\text{ K}$ (typical Ka-band LNA noise figure $\approx 3\text{ dB}$), $T_{cable} \approx 50\text{ K}$ → $T_{sys} \approx 280\text{ K}$.

In dBm for $B = 100\text{ kHz}$:

$$N = 10\log_{10}(1.38\times10^{-23} \times 280 \times 10^5) + 30 = -114.1\text{ dBm}$$

The **noise spectral density** $N_0 = k_B T_{sys}$ sets the fundamental limit:

$$N_0 = 1.38\times10^{-23} \times 280 = 3.86\times10^{-21}\text{ W/Hz} = -174.1\text{ dBm/Hz}$$

---

### 3. Complete Link Budget

$$\text{SNR}(\text{dB}) = P_{tx} + G_{tx} + G_{rx} - L_{fs} - L_{point} - N$$

$$\frac{E_b}{N_0}(\text{dB}) = \text{SNR}(\text{dB}) + 10\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{B}{R_b}\right)$$

For **SISP baseline** ($d = 1000\text{ km}$, $B = 100\text{ kHz}$, $R_b = 100\text{ kbps}$, so $B/R_b = 1$ → $0\text{ dB}$ bandwidth factor):

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| $P_{tx}$ | $+30\text{ dBm}$ | 1 W |
| $G_{tx}$ | $+10\text{ dBi}$ | Patch phased array |
| $G_{rx}$ | $+10\text{ dBi}$ | Same |
| $L_{fs}$ | $-144.8\text{ dB}$ | 1000 km, 26 GHz |
| $L_{point}$ | $-2\text{ dB}$ | Pointing loss estimate |
| $N$ | $-114.1\text{ dBm}$ | 100 kHz, 280 K |
| **SNR** | **$+17.3\text{ dB}$** | Comfortable |
| **$E_b/N_0$** | **$+17.3\text{ dB}$** | $B = R_b$ |

At 1 MHz bandwidth (poor design choice): $N = -114+10 = -104\text{ dBm}$ → SNR drops to $+7.3\text{ dB}$.

**Key insight**: The choice of $B = 100\text{ kHz}$ (matched to the bit rate) gives a 10 dB advantage over the 1 MHz estimate in the literature review. This is why the link works well.

---

### 4. BER Analysis — Three Regimes

#### 4.1 Uncoded BPSK

The optimal coherent receiver computes the decision variable:

$$z = \int_0^{T_b} r(t)\cdot\sqrt{2}\cos(2\pi f_c t)\,dt$$

and decides $\hat{b} = +1$ if $z > 0$, else $-1$. The BER is:

$$\text{BER}_{\text{BPSK}} = Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{2E_b}{N_0}}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\text{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{E_b}{N_0}}\right)$$

where $Q(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_x^\infty e^{-t^2/2}dt$ and $\text{erfc}(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_x^\infty e^{-t^2}dt$.

Key values:

| $E_b/N_0$ (dB) | $E_b/N_0$ (linear) | BER (uncoded) |
|---|---|---|
| 4 | 2.51 | $6.8\times10^{-3}$ |
| 6 | 3.98 | $1.3\times10^{-3}$ |
| 8 | 6.31 | $1.1\times10^{-4}$ |
| 10 | 10.0 | $3.9\times10^{-6}$ |
| 12 | 15.8 | $5.3\times10^{-8}$ |
| 17.3 | 53.7 | $\approx 10^{-27}$ |

At the SISP baseline ($E_b/N_0 = 17.3\text{ dB}$), uncoded BPSK is already essentially perfect. The coding discussion below is for the **minimum viable range** (long distance, low SNR).

#### 4.2 Rate-1/2 Convolutional Code (Soft-Decision Viterbi)

The encoder maps each input bit to 2 coded bits using a generator polynomial. Standard NASA-standard rate-1/2 K=7 convolutional code: generators $g_1 = 1011011$, $g_2 = 1111001$ (octal: 133, 171).

The BER after soft-decision Viterbi decoding is bounded by:

$$\text{BER}_{\text{conv}} \leq \frac{1}{k}\sum_{d=d_{\text{free}}}^\infty c_d \cdot Q\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{2d \cdot E_b}{N_0}\cdot R_c}\right)$$

where $d_{\text{free}} = 10$ (free distance of K=7 rate-1/2), $R_c = 1/2$ is the code rate, $k=1$ input bit per trellis step, and $c_d$ are the weight enumerator coefficients. For engineering use, the tight approximation:

$$\text{BER}_{\text{conv}} \approx \frac{1}{2}\text{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{E_b}{N_0}\cdot R_c \cdot d_{\text{free}}}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\text{erfc}\!\left(\sqrt{5\,\frac{E_b}{N_0}}\right)$$

The **coding gain** is approximately $10\log_{10}(R_c \cdot d_{\text{free}}) = 10\log_{10}(5) = 7\text{ dB}$ at BER $= 10^{-5}$. In practice it's $\approx 5.5\text{ dB}$ at $10^{-3}$ (the rate-effective penalty).

#### 4.3 Outer Reed-Solomon Code RS(255,223) over GF(2⁸)

The RS outer code operates on **bytes** (symbols), correcting up to $t = 16$ byte errors per codeword of 255 bytes (223 information bytes + 32 redundancy bytes). Code rate $R_{RS} = 223/255 = 0.875$.

After the convolutional inner decoder, the **byte error rate** entering the RS decoder is:

$$P_{byte} = 1 - (1 - \text{BER}_{\text{conv}})^8 \approx 8 \cdot \text{BER}_{\text{conv}}$$

The RS decoder corrects all patterns with $\leq t = 16$ symbol errors per block. The residual byte error rate after RS decoding:

$$P_{RS} \approx \binom{255}{t+1} P_{byte}^{t+1} (1-P_{byte})^{254-t}$$

For $\text{BER}_{\text{conv}} = 10^{-4}$ (corresponding to $E_b/N_0 \approx 5\text{ dB}$ after convolutional coding):

$$P_{byte} \approx 8\times10^{-4}, \quad P_{RS} \approx \binom{255}{17}(8\times10^{-4})^{17} \approx 10^{-32}$$

The concatenated scheme (conv + RS) is essentially **perfect** for $E_b/N_0 \geq 4.5\text{ dB}$. The total code rate: $R_{total} = R_c \times R_{RS} = 0.5 \times 0.875 = 0.4375$.

---

### 5. Packet Error Rate (PER) for SISP Messages

Given a coded BER $p_b$, the probability that an $L$-bit packet contains **at least one uncorrected bit error**:

$$\text{PER}(L, p_b) = 1 - (1 - p_b)^L$$

For small $p_b$: $\text{PER} \approx L \cdot p_b$.

SISP packet sizes (after encoding):

| Message type | Payload (bytes) | Total uncoded bits | Coded bits (×2.29) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Header only | 5 | 40 | 92 |
| CORRECTION_REQ | 5 + 12 = 17 | 136 | 311 |
| CORRECTION_RSP | 5 + 12 = 17 | 136 | 311 |
| RELAY_REQ | 5 + 100 = 105 | 840 | 1924 |
| BORROW_DATA | 5 + 1024 = 1029 | 8232 | 18852 |

The **minimum viable $E_b/N_0$** is defined as the point where $\text{PER} < 10^{-4}$ for a CORRECTION message (311 coded bits). For the concatenated scheme, this occurs at:

$$\left.\frac{E_b}{N_0}\right\vert_{\text{min viable}} \approx 4.5\text{ dB (concatenated)} \quad \text{vs} \quad 8\text{ dB (uncoded)}$$

---

### 6. Maximum Service Range — The Neighborhood Radius

Inverting the link budget to find $d_{\max}$ given minimum $E_b/N_0$:

$$d_{\max} = \frac{c}{4\pi f_c}\sqrt{\frac{P_{tx} G_{tx} G_{rx}}{k_B T_{sys} R_b \cdot (E_b/N_0)_{\min}}}$$

At $(E_b/N_0)_{\min} = 4.5\text{ dB} = 2.82$ (concatenated coding), $R_b = 100\text{ kbps}$:

$$d_{\max} = \frac{3\times10^8}{4\pi\times2.6\times10^{10}}\sqrt{\frac{1 \times 10 \times 10}{1.38\times10^{-23}\times280\times10^5\times2.82}}$$

$$= \frac{3\times10^8}{3.27\times10^{11}}\sqrt{\frac{100}{1.09\times10^{-14}}} = 9.17\times10^{-4}\times\sqrt{9.17\times10^{15}}$$

$$= 9.17\times10^{-4}\times 9.58\times10^7 \approx \mathbf{87{,}900\text{ km}}$$

This far exceeds LEO constellation spacing — meaning the physical layer is **not the limiting factor** for Service 1. The actual neighborhood radius is determined by **orbital geometry** (visibility windows, shared footprint) from Module M1, not by link budget.

For UHF (437 MHz, $G_{tx} = G_{rx} = 2\text{ dBi}$):

$$d_{\max}^{\text{UHF}} \approx 3{,}200\text{ km}$$

Still larger than typical LEO neighbor spacing ($\leq 2000\text{ km}$). SISP works on **both** Ka-band and UHF — important resilience property.

**Plot 3 — PER vs distance tab**: Change the message type selector to compare CORRECTION_REQ vs BORROW_DATA. The BORROW_DATA packet (18852 coded bits) degrades PER much faster with distance — this directly motivates the **RELAY service**: large payloads need a relay hop, while small correction messages can traverse longer distances directly.
